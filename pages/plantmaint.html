Choosing The Right Pot Material

The material of your pots plays an important role in how your plants grow. Porous options like terracotta and ceramic let moisture escape faster, which can help prevent mold in rainy areas but may require extra watering in hot, dry climates. On the other hand, recycled plastic or metal containers hold moisture longer and are lighter to move, making them great for hanging setups or frequently rearranged gardens.


Container-friendly Plants


Rudbeckia

Rudbeckia brings bold color to container gardens, offering long-lasting blooms that stand out even in compact spaces. Its sturdy nature makes it reliable through heat and moderate drought, without needing constant care. As a native plant, it provides essential nectar for bees and butterflies, adding ecological value beyond its beauty.

Coreopsis

Coreopsis is a native plant that adapts well to containers, making it perfect for small gardens, patios, and balconies. It thrives with minimal care, attracts pollinators, and blooms brightly through the season. Because it's native, it supports local ecosystems while requiring less water and maintenance.

Weed Control

Weed control is an important part of maintaining native plants because it helps protect their space and resources. Even though native plants are hardy, weeds can quickly spread and make it harder for them to grow properly.

Keeping Weeds Away

Weeds compete with native plants for sunlight, water, and nutrients. By removing weeds regularly, you give native plants the space and resources they need to thrive. Simple methods like hand-pulling, adding mulch, or checking the area each week can keep weeds under control without harming the plants or the environment.

Common Mistakes

Transitioning to a native garden requires a shift in mindset. While native plants are resilient, they have different needs than traditional ornamental species. Avoiding these three common pitfalls will ensure your indigenous trees and plants thrive.

Over-Amending Soil

Many gardeners habitually add rich compost or fertilizer before planting. However, native plants are adapted to local, often nutrient-poor soils. Adding too much fertilizer can actually cause natives to grow tall and floppy, or encourage weeds to outcompete them.

"Tidying" Too Much

Resist the urge to cut everything down in the fall. Hollow stems and leaf litter provide essential winter shelter for hibernating bees, butterflies, and caterpillars. A "messy" garden is a living winter habitat for the biodiversity you are trying to protect.

Wrong Plant, Wrong Place

"Native" doesn't mean a plant will grow anywhere in your region. A wetland native will die in a dry, sunny spot. You must still match the specific plant to your garden's sunlight and soil moisture conditions to ensure it establishes deep, healthy roots.
