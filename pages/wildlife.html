Benefits Native Plants Bring

Native plants offer a range of environmental and community benefits that support both people and the local ecosystem.

Environmental Benefits

Native plants offer important environmental benefits because they are naturally adapted to their local ecosystems. They require less water, fertilizer, and maintenance, which helps conserve resources and reduce pollution. Their deep root systems improve soil health, prevent erosion, and help rainwater soak into the ground.

Community Benefits

Native plants strengthen communities by bringing a sense of place and identity to shared environments. Their familiar shapes, colours, and seasonal changes create natural beauty that reflects the region’s character. Because they are suited to the local climate, their shade stays healthy and reliable along walking paths. Many native species also provide edible fruits, leaves, or materials traditionally used by local people.

Long-term Sustainibility

Native plants offer long-term sustainability because they are naturally suited to the local climate, allowing them to thrive while using less water. Their built-in resistance to local pests reduces the need for pesticides, keeping soil and waterways cleaner. Over time, they help restore ecosystems by supporting native animals and maintaining balanced habitats.

Native Plants support Biodiversity

Native trees play a major role in supporting biodiversity and wildlife because they are naturally adapted to the local environment. They provide the specific types of food—such as leaves, nectar, fruits, and seeds—that local insects, birds, and animals rely on. Native trees also create essential habitats, offering nesting places, shelter, and materials for many species.

Their root systems help maintain healthy soil by preventing erosion and supporting organisms like fungi and microbes, which further boosts ecosystem health.

Native Plants Support Wildlife

Native plants support biodiversity by providing the food, shelter, and breeding habitats that local wildlife need to survive. They attract insects, which feed birds and other animals, and their roots and leaf litter help maintain healthy soil and water systems. By sustaining these ecological relationships, native plants create balanced and resilient ecosystems.

The Impact of Quercus (Oaks)

1

Oaks are considered one of the most important “keystone” native trees in the world, especially across North America, Europe, and parts of Asia. Their ecological benefits are unusually large compared to most other trees.

2

Native oaks support over 500 species of caterpillars (Lepidoptera), far more than most other tree genera. This huge insect biomass forms the base of local food webs. Because baby birds depend on soft-bodied insects, oak forests allow birds to successfully breed. For example, a single brood of chickadees can require 6,000–9,000 caterpillars — something only native keystone trees like oaks can supply.

3

Their complex branching structure provides nesting sites for large and small birds. Cavities in older oaks support bats, owls, woodpeckers, and small mammals like squirrels. Fallen leaves and acorns form micro-habitats for reptiles, amphibians, and decomposers.

4

Oaks have deep root systems that anchor soil and reduce erosion, while their broad canopies moderate ground temperature and protect understory plants. Their long lifespans also allow them to store large amounts of carbon, making them important contributors to climate regulation and ecosystem stability.
